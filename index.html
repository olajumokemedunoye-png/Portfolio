Case Study Deep Dives

💰 Financial Services
Workforce Cost-of-Turnover & Attrition Risk Model
🔍 The Challenge

A mid-sized financial services organisation was experiencing a persistent 22% annual attrition rate — but leadership had no visibility into where exits were concentrated, why they were happening, or what they were truly costing the business. HR were producing basic headcount reports, but no one could quantify the financial consequence of losing a mid-level analyst versus a frontline processor. Decisions were reactive and anecdotal.

⚙️ The Approach

I built a multi-dimensional attrition model in Excel and Power BI, pulling together tenure data, performance ratings, department codes, and exit interview themes. I segmented the workforce into tenure bands (0–6m, 6–18m, 18m–3yr, 3yr+) and overlaid exit rates per band. Using a cost-of-turnover formula (recruitment cost + onboarding time + productivity ramp + manager bandwidth), I assigned a financial value to each exit by role grade. SQL queries were used to extract clean headcount snapshots from the HRIS on a monthly cadence.

💡 Key Findings & Recommendations

The model revealed that 68% of all exits occurred within the first 18 months — pointing clearly to an onboarding and early engagement failure rather than a compensation issue. The highest financial impact was in mid-senior analyst roles, where each exit cost an estimated £18,500 in total. I recommended a structured 90-day check-in programme, a revised onboarding experience for months 3–12, and early-tenure manager accountability metrics.

💻 Technology
Headcount Forecasting & Capacity Planning Model
🔍 The Challenge

A scaling technology business was preparing for a Series B growth phase, planning to grow headcount from approximately 180 FTE to 420 FTE over 18 months. Their People team was working from static spreadsheets with no dynamic scenario modelling. As each hire happened, the spreadsheet broke. Finance and HR were misaligned on hiring timelines, and the CFO had no reliable view of future payroll liability across growth scenarios.

⚙️ The Approach

I designed a dynamic headcount planning model in Excel with three growth scenarios — Conservative (15% growth), Base (28% growth), and Accelerated (42% growth). Each scenario mapped hiring by quarter, role type, salary band, and department. Power BI dashboards connected to the model provided a real-time view of planned vs actual headcount, open requisitions, and rolling 12-month payroll cost projections. PostgreSQL was used to automate the pull of live headcount from their HRIS into the model.

💡 Key Findings & Recommendations

The modelling exposed a critical dependency risk — 61% of planned hires were concentrated in two departments, creating both a talent pipeline bottleneck and a single-point-of-failure for growth delivery. I recommended hiring sequencing changes and introduced a role criticality score to prioritise requisitions. The model was formally adopted for quarterly board reporting.

🏥 Care & Healthcare
Staffing Ratio, Burnout Risk & Absence Forecasting
🔍 The Challenge

A care provider operating across multiple sites was facing a mounting crisis: reactive agency spend had risen 34% year-on-year as managers scrambled to fill last-minute gaps caused by unplanned absence. There was no early-warning system. Burnout was visible only after someone resigned or went on extended sick leave. The finance team had no forecasting model to manage agency costs, and the operations team were firefighting rather than planning.

⚙️ The Approach

I built a staffing load and absence trend model integrating rota data, Bradford Factor absence scores, shift patterns, and patient volume forecasts. A Power BI dashboard flagged individuals and teams exceeding the burnout risk threshold — defined as Bradford Factor above 200 combined with 3+ consecutive short-absence spells. Six-week forward-looking staffing forecasts were produced from shift data, enabling proactive rota planning. Excel was used for the base model; SQL queries extracted absence records from the HR system.

💡 Key Findings & Recommendations

The model identified that Monday and Friday shifts carried 3x the absence risk of mid-week — a pattern invisible in the previous reporting. Night shift workers in the 2–3 year tenure band showed the highest burnout indicators. I recommended a targeted wellbeing check-in programme for flagged individuals, a revised rota design to distribute high-pressure shifts, and a 6-week rolling absence forecast embedded into the monthly ops review.

📞 Contact Centre / Operations
Customer Operations KPI & SLA Performance Dashboard
🔍 The Challenge

A customer operations team managing 12,000+ monthly support tickets was producing performance reports manually from three disconnected data sources — a CRM, Jira, and a spreadsheet log. The process took 2–3 days per reporting cycle. By the time leadership received the data, it was already outdated. SLA breaches were being discovered after the fact, CSAT dips had no root cause analysis attached, and agent performance comparisons were unreliable due to inconsistent data handling.

⚙️ The Approach

I designed a unified KPI architecture, mapping 14 metrics across four performance dimensions: Speed (First Response Time, Resolution Time), Quality (CSAT, QA Score), Compliance (SLA Breach Rate, Escalation Rate), and Productivity (Tickets per Agent, Reopen Rate). PostgreSQL queries automated the extraction and transformation of ticket data from the CRM and Jira databases into a clean reporting layer. Power BI dashboards refreshed daily, providing a single source of truth with drill-through capability to individual agent and ticket level.

💡 Key Findings & Recommendations

The dashboard immediately surfaced that the top 3 complaint drivers — billing errors, delivery delays, and account access — accounted for 74% of all SLA breaches. These were systemic process issues, not agent skill gaps. I presented this finding with a recommendation to route these ticket types to a specialist handling team, which was implemented. CSAT improved by 11 points over the following quarter. Reporting time dropped from 3 days to 4 hours.
